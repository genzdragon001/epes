                             INTRODUCTION

The Background of the Study

         In the global landscape of higher education, deans are playing an important role in
achieving the quality of education, development of faculty and academic excellence.
However these deans nowadays are facing a lot of challenges in managing their faculty
performance amid of rising expectations regarding accountability, transparency, and data-
based decision making. Nkata, J. L., & Ssempebwa, J. (2024), Makerere University,
Uganda, highlighted the strenuous nature of deans' roles, emphasizing the difficulty in
balancing faculty and administrative demands. Deans often find themselves caught
between the expectations of faculty and the directives of the central administration, leading
to role conflict and ambiguity. Hussain et al. (2025), said that to be fair, consistent, and
successful, performance review systems need to be both quantitative and qualitative.

         In the case of Philippine Higher education, these difficulties are worsen because of
the limited digital resources, administrative inefficiencies, and inconsistent standards in
faculty evaluation practices. The Second Congressional Commission on Education
(EDCOM 2) highlighted in its 2025 report that many State Universities and Colleges
(SUCs) continue to rely on the outdated and fragmented systems, which lead to weak
governance and poor data utilization. The Philippine Institute for Development Studies
(PIDS) further observed that despite the increasing enrollees in the higher education, the
quality assurance and institutional management is still inconsistent, with some SUC are
still struggling with the implementation of strong and effective systems for evaluation
success.

         Colleges and universities outside of major cities face additional challenges, such as
perceived biases, a lack of transparency in the evaluation process, and delays in faculty
performance feedback (Abejo, 2024). Many of these schools lack clear guidelines for
research and evaluation, provide teachers with excessive work outside of class, and have
even fewer resources, which demotivates teachers and hinders the success of the school
(Dela Cruz & Dela Cruz, 2024). Additionally, a large number of regional institutions still
conduct faculty reviews using manual, paper-based processes, which exacerbates
inefficiencies and delays crucial administrative decisions (Loyola, 2023). Therefore,
regional higher education institutions face even more serious issues like a lack of resources
and unclear procedures in addition to the same issues facing the rest of the nation, such as
inconsistent standards and weak leadership (Abejo, 2024; Dela Cruz & Dela Cruz, 2024;
Loyola, 2023).

         At the institutional level, Dr. Emilio B. Espinosa Sr. Memorial State College of
Agriculture and Technology (DEBESMSCAT) is also facing this kind of challenges. One
of the most urgent issues is the manual processing and reconciliation of faculty
performance data, which is time-consuming, prone to error, and lacks transparency. This
method contributes to delays in evaluation, inconsistencies in documentation, and
difficulties in aligning faculty outputs with institutional priorities. As a result, not only are
deans burdened with administrative workload, but overall academic planning, resource
allocation, and institutional reporting are also compromised.
         The Individual Performance Commitment and Review (IPCR), is a standard tool
required by the Civil Service Commission under the Strategic Performance Management
System (SPMS). It is an essential component of faculty performance evaluation in every
SUC. Part of the IPCR process is setting performance goals, keeping track of
accomplishments, and evaluating results based on Office Performance Commitment and
Review (OPCR) goals. But there are big problems in preparing IPCRs manually, such as
duplication of data, lack of real time monitoring, and having trouble in compiling all reports
for submission. These issues make it harder to keep track of success and make decisions.

         These challenges are not exclusive to DEBESMSCAT. Other Philippine
institutions report similar issues. For instance, a study at Pangasinan State University found
that faculty members with multiple roles struggled with preparing IPCRs due to
overlapping responsibilities and the absence of standardized performance computation
models. The study proposed a mathematical model, evaluated using the Technology
Acceptance Model, which significantly improved user satisfaction (Cenas, 2022).
Similarly, at Leyte Normal University, researchers observed that although faculty
performance ratings were generally satisfactory, the process heavily depended on manual
inputs, which affected the reliability and consistency of results (Caluza et al., 2017).

         The IPCR workflow typically begins with setting individual targets aligned with
the institution's OPCR. Faculty members then document their accomplishments
throughout the performance period, which are validated by their respective deans and
submitted to the Performance Management Team. Key performance areas--namely
instruction, research, extension, and production--carry varying weights, with instruction
often taking precedence due to its direct link to student outcomes and accreditation
requirements.

         Given the systemic and institutional inefficiencies, there is a clear and urgent need
for a digital solution that streamlines faculty performance documentation and evaluation,
reduces administrative burden, and enhances data accuracy and usability. In response, this
capstone project proposes the design and development of a Faculty Performance
Documentation and Evaluation System tailored to DEBESMSCAT's operational context,
while aligning with national standards such as SPMS and OPCR/IPCR protocols. The
system aims to support evidence-based academic planning, foster transparency, and
empower academic leaders in making informed decisions.

         DEBESMSCAT quality policy and support institutional goals.

Problem/Opportunity Definition

         At DEBESMSCAT, faculty members and deans face difficulties with regards to
performance data reconciliation, IPCR documents consolidation, and aligning outputs with
institutional goals. The absence of a centralized digital system results in redundant
documentation, limited access to historical data, and challenges in consolidating reports
for OPCR submissions. This limits the institution in making more informed decisions,
allocating resources and strategic planning.

         Despite the digital technologies availability, currently DEBESMSCAT has no
system that would address this needs of faculty performance documentation and
evaluation. Also, the lack of innovations like this limits the potential for intellectual
Property production, which could contribute to institutional goals.

Specifically, it seeks to answer the following questions:

    1. What are the limitations of the current manual system used for faculty performance
         documentation and IPCR preparation at DEBESMSCAT?

    2. How can the proposed system improve the efficiency, accuracy, and acceptability
         of faculty performance evaluation among stakeholders?

    3. What innovative features can be integrated into the system to qualify it for
         intellectual property protection?

General Objective:
         The general objective of this capstone project is to design and develop a Faculty

Performance Documentation and Evaluation System for DEBESMSCAT that facilitates
evidence-based decision-making by automating the collection, analysis, and reporting of
faculty performance data. Specifically, it aims to:
    1. To develop a functional web-based system that enables efficient documentation,
         monitoring, and evaluation of faculty performance based on predefined institutional
         criteria and metrics.

    2. To assess the usability, functionality, and overall acceptability of the developed
         system among faculty members through user testing and feedback mechanisms.

    3. To create a patent draft document that outlines the unique features and technical
         specifications of the developed system.

Significance of the Study
         This study is significant as it addresses a critical gap in the performance evaluation

process of faculty members within State Universities and Colleges (SUCs), particularly
at DEBESMSCAT. By designing and developing the system tailored to the documentation

institutional processes and supports the broader goal of evidence-based decision-making
in higher education.

1. For the Institution (DEBESMSCAT): The system will help the institution's
    efficiency by automating the preparation and consolidation of IPCR reports. This will
    reduce the burden on deans and faculty, reduce errors, and improve the timeliness and

2. To the Faculty Members: All Faculty will benefit from an efficient and transparent

4. To the Higher Education Sector: The study contributes to the ongoing digital
    transformation of SUCs in the Philippines. It aligns with national efforts to improve
    governance, transparency, and quality assurance in higher education, as emphasized by
    EDCOM 2 and the Civil Service Commission.

5. To Future Researchers and Developers: This project serves as a foundation for future
    innovations in academic performance management systems. It opens opportunities for

    not only adds value to the institution but also promotes innovation and recognition in

The scope of the Study

         This study focuses on the design and development of a Faculty Performance
Documentation and Evaluation System specifically tailored for Dr. Emilio B. Espinosa Sr.
Memorial State College of Agriculture and Technology (DEBESMSCAT). The system
aims to automate and streamline the preparation, submission, and evaluation of
the Individual Performance Commitment and Review (IPCR) and Office Performance
Commitment and Review (OPCR) forms.

The system will include the following core features:

     Digital encoding and submission of IPCR data
     Manual input of ratings per performance criterion by authorized evaluators
     Automated computation of the overall performance rating based on weighted

         criteria
     Real-time tracking of faculty accomplishments
     Role-based access for faculty, deans, and staff.
     Report generation for performance summaries and OPCR consolidation
     Basic analytics to support evidence-based decision-making

The system will be deployed at the College of Arts and Sciences, DEBESMSCAT and will
primarily serve Faculty and Dean.

Delimitations of the Study
     The system is developed and tested only within DEBESMSCAT, and may not
         immediately reflect the needs or workflows of other SUCs or private institutions.
     While individual criterion ratings are manually inputted, the system automatically
         calculates the overall performance rating to ensure consistency and reduce human
         error.
     The evaluation will focus on instructional, research, extension, and production
         functions as outlined in the IPCR, but will not include other HR-related modules
         such as payroll, leave management, or promotion tracking.

                REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE AND SYSTEMS

Related Literature
         The growing emphasis on evidence-based decision-making in higher education has

transformed the way institutions evaluate faculty performance. The literature reveals how
academic organizations worldwide are adapting to new methods of documentation,
automation, and holistic assessment. This synthesis categorizes relevant research into four
major themes, aligned with the goals of your proposed Faculty Performance
Documentation and Evaluation System.

Data-Driven and Contextualized Evaluation Models
         Globally, there is a significant shift toward data-driven and institution-specific

faculty evaluation systems. Hussain, Lee, and Patel (2025) highlighted the role of analytics-
powered platforms in delivering personalized assessments that balance transparency and
academic rigor. Wang (2023) illustrated how cultural and institutional contexts influence
evaluation approaches in elite institutions such as Harvard and Peking University. Santiago
and Benavides (2009) and Isoré (2009) both cautioned against rigid, standardized models,
instead recommending adaptive frameworks suited to individual institutions. Khan (2013)
advocated for developmental evaluations that foster faculty growth rather than impose
punitive measures. Complementing these conceptual insights, Benosa and Ońate (2023)
and Dela Fuente and Dela Fuente (2022) introduced analytics-integrated platforms that
empower institutional decision-making. Meanwhile, Rapacke (2024), Thompson (2025),
and Tinch (2025) emphasized the need for originality and innovation in system design to
meet the technical requirements for intellectual property eligibility.

         In the Philippine context, studies similarly call for automated and analytics-
enhanced evaluation systems adapted to local needs. Bueno (2023) reported inconsistent
faculty evaluations at Columban College due to the lack of standard tools. Cenas (2022)
responded to the complexity of multiple faculty roles by proposing a mathematical model
for IPCR accuracy validated using the Technology Acceptance Model. Dela Fuente and
Dela Fuente (2022) demonstrated how analytics integration improved assessment
reliability and minimized human error. At ISPSC, Cinizan and Valdez (2023) enhanced
their Faculty Performance Evaluation System by embedding analytics features, achieving
a very high usability rating. Romblon State University implemented intelligent evaluation
mechanisms such as Common Criteria Evaluation (CCE) and Qualitative Contribution
Evaluation (QCE), supported by analytics to streamline assessments. The Polytechnic
University of the Philippines linked online evaluation results to teaching load and faculty
promotions, reinforcing performance-based advancement. National frameworks such as
CHED's Institutional Sustainability Assessment (ISA) toolkit and Self-Evaluation
Document (SED), along with DepEd's IPCRF system and the Civil Service Commission's
Strategic Performance Management System (SPMS), collectively reflect a growing trend
toward objective, data-backed, and context-aware faculty evaluations in the country.

Holistic and Multifaceted Faculty Evaluation
         Foreign scholars increasingly advocate for comprehensive faculty evaluation

systems that consider not just instructional effectiveness but also research productivity,
service engagement, and professional development. Kezar (2018) argued for aligning
evaluations with institutional missions and faculty roles, while Darling-Hammond (2013)
promoted formative evaluations as tools for professional growth. Weaver and Carlisle
(2023) offered implementation strategies for such holistic models, and Kumar (2025)
discussed India's UGC framework that ensures equal weight across instruction, research,
and service. Brown (2015) and Golde and Dore (2001) highlighted the disconnect between
expectations and faculty preparation, proposing feedback systems to bridge the gap.
Additionally, Lohman (2021), Bernstein (2008), and Boyer (1996) supported integrating
mentoring, peer review, and scholarly identity into evaluation structures to ensure
meaningful, development-oriented assessments.

         Locally, multiple studies reflect this call for more holistic and multidimensional
faculty evaluations. Caluza et al. (2017) found that the reliability of faculty assessment at
Leyte Normal University was compromised by manual input processes, advocating for
digital systems that integrate multiple indicators. Patimo (2020) observed that while SUCs
in Eastern Visayas employed rating scales, they often lacked automation and qualitative
assessment tools. EDCOM 2 (2024) highlighted faculty evaluation as essential for
promoting both institutional growth and individual professional development. CHED's
ISA framework positioned faculty evaluation as a cornerstone of institutional
sustainability, focusing on quality teaching, governance, and community involvement.
DepEd's Results-Based Performance Management System (RPMS) and IPCRF modules
emphasized the importance of development planning and formative feedback. The CSC's
SPMS Guidebook introduced a four-stage cycle--planning, monitoring, review, and
rewarding--designed to support continuous improvement. Likewise, evaluation manuals
from ASSCAT and PCSO link performance assessments to staff incentives and career
development, while CHED Region 2 provided templates that help institutions align
evaluations with broader academic goals.

Usability and System Innovation
         The usability of faculty evaluation systems is a critical factor in their success, and

international research has emphasized the importance of user-centered design (UCD)
principles. Cruz et al. (2021) demonstrated that UCD significantly enhances user adoption
and long-term engagement with performance platforms, a conclusion further supported by
Alao et al. (2022) through practical application in university systems. Yesa, Putri, and Noer
(2024), Sani et al. (2023), and Orallo (2025) all highlighted how intuitive, design-thinking-
based interfaces on academic websites and mobile applications improve efficiency and
accessibility, ultimately encouraging more meaningful participation from users.

         In the local setting, several Philippine institutions have demonstrated how system
innovation and usability improvements lead to better performance monitoring. Tarlac State
University's Automated Faculty Evaluation System improved transparency and reduced
administrative workload through an intuitive design. At ISPSC, the enhanced FPES
utilized Agile development and data analytics to achieve high usability scores. Central
Mindanao University's system automated faculty performance monitoring and supported
multi-source feedback mechanisms. NVSU developed a web and mobile-based platform
that enabled students to evaluate faculty and access real-time performance dashboards.
Salas (2015) built a mobile-responsive evaluation system that received strong ratings for
functionality and portability. Plata and Facun (2020) implemented a paperless feedback
platform that simplified the student evaluation process. Ramos et al. (2020) designed a
LAN-based appraisal system that improved documentation efficiency and reduced
evaluation time. Celis and Pagatpatan (2019) applied the Analytic Hierarchy Process
(AHP) algorithm to increase the accuracy of evaluation decision-making, while Fadera and
Enriquez (2018) integrated intelligent analytics into their systems to accelerate data
processing and reduce errors.

IP Eligibility and System Originality
         The global literature underscores the importance of originality and technical

innovation in creating systems that are eligible for intellectual property (IP) protection.
Rapacke (2024) explained that systems must solve identifiable technical problems and
incorporate method-based uniqueness to qualify for protection. Thompson (2025)
discussed how the Alice Two-Part Test determines whether a system's features go beyond
abstract ideas, which is essential for patentability. Tinch (2025) emphasized innovations
such as improved interactivity and novel analytics as enhancements that support IP claims.
Collins (2024) and InspireIP (2025) both reinforced that comprehensive documentation
and strategic technological advancements are fundamental to obtaining software IP rights.
ISO (2025) highlighted the role of security standards and originality criteria in
safeguarding digital systems from replication and unauthorized use.

         In the Philippines, recent developments also reflect efforts to build evaluation
systems that meet the standards of IP eligibility. Dela Fuente and Dela Fuente (2022)
highlighted that their analytics-based system was designed with original features tailored
to institutional faculty evaluation, making it a candidate for IP registration. Cinizan and
Valdez (2023) documented the unique development process of their FPES, which included
modular architecture and advanced data visualization components. Romblon State
University integrated custom algorithms and analytics dashboards to strengthen the
system's claim to technical originality. CHED's ISA toolkit further encourages higher
education institutions to innovate and document their internal quality assurance
mechanisms--including faculty evaluation platforms--as a step toward institutional
development and intellectual property recognition.

Related Systems
Automated and Analytics-Driven Systems

         Globally, institutions are embracing systems that automate faculty evaluation while
integrating powerful analytics. Tools like Cflow Faculty Evaluation Platform and EduRate
utilize AI features--such as OCR, decision trees, and data visualization--to generate
performance insights. Multi-criteria decision-making models like AHP, TOPSIS, and Grey
MARCOS enhance objectivity across complex faculty roles. These systems exemplify how
automation and data science can refine evaluation practices and support evidence-based
administration.

         In the Philippine context, SUCs are rapidly adopting similar tools. ISPSC's
enhanced Faculty Performance Evaluation System uses analytics and Agile methods to
streamline reviews and received high usability marks. Romblon State University's
intelligent evaluation model applies Common Criteria and Qualitative Contribution
Evaluation to quantify faculty output. DepEd's IPCRF templates and CHED's ISA
framework emphasize digital tracking and strategic alignment. These local systems mirror
global trends while addressing region-specific challenges like multi-role faculty and
resource constraints.

Usability and Interface-Focused Systems
         International studies highlight how user-centered design principles boost system

adoption in academic settings. Platforms such as Visual Analytics for Teaching Practice
and dashboards embedded in EduRate rely on intuitive navigation, real-time feedback, and
mobile responsiveness to encourage engagement. AHP-based decision support and clean
design layouts help stakeholders provide input easily and interpret results quickly.

         Filipino institutions are equally focused on interface design that caters to users.
Tarlac State University's AFES features student-facing tools, checkbox evaluations, and
streamlined reporting. Central Mindanao University and NVSU use mobile-accessible
systems with dashboards tailored for faculty, admins, and students. Systems developed by
Ramos, Plata, Salas, and Celis prioritized intuitive design, portability, and data reliability.
The emphasis on ease-of-use aligns Philippine innovations with global best practices,
demonstrating that user experience is as vital as analytical power in faculty evaluation.

IP-Eligible and Innovative Systems
         Across foreign literature, originality in system architecture is key to achieving

intellectual property protection. Researchers emphasize patentability through modular
design, unique algorithms, and documented innovation. Platforms like InspireIP and ISO-
compliant models ensure legal eligibility by capturing inventive technical solutions and
safeguarding information security. Rapacke, Thompson, and Tinch outline the legal
frameworks--such as the Alice Two-Part Test--that systems must satisfy to be protected
under IP law.

         Philippine developers are rising to the challenge of creating novel systems eligible
for IP registration. ISPSC and Romblon State University embedded distinct analytics
engines, modular structures, and custom evaluation workflows into their platforms.
Documentation efforts and Agile-based development processes showcase originality.
CHED's ISA guidance encourages institutions to treat internal quality assurance tools as
potentially proprietary assets. The innovative use of algorithms like AHP for faculty
ranking further bolsters the claim for IP protection. This shows how Philippine systems are
not just following global standards--they're carving out their own space in the realm of
educational software innovation.

Analysis and Design
         People Analysis: The current Faculty Performance Evaluation process involves

multiple stakeholders across academic and administrative tasks. It includes the Faculty
members who prepared and submit Individual Performance Commitment and Review
(IPCR) forms, Program Heads and Supervisors who reviews submissions and assign
ratings, and Deans who oversee College performance and consolidates evaluation results.

         Process Analysis: The existing process flow is manual and paper-based, beginning
with the cascading of targets, distribution of IPCR templates, followed by individual
preparation of reports, physical submissions, and manual checking of supervisors. The
ratings are consolidated using spreadsheets and printed for administrative review. This
process is prone to delays, data entry errors, and inconsistent data formatting, especially
during evaluation periods. Decision points ­ such as report validation, rating approval, and
document generations are often bottlenecks due to limited coordination and lack of real
time visibility. The absence of centralized tracking make it difficult to monitor submissions
status, identify missing reports, or generate summaries on time.

         A digital system with automated workflows, submission notification, and users
dashboards will streamline this process, reduce redundancy, and improve accountability.

         Technology Analysis: The College is still relying on MS Excel and email for
performance documentation, without centralized database platform. Faculty reports are
stored in local drives or printed archives, which results to labor intensive when retrieve.
The network infrastructure supports browser-based access and cloud hosting, but lacks
dedicated systems for performance management. Existing hardware--faculty desktops and
admin workstations--is sufficient for web-based applications, though some upgrades may
be required for older devices. The proposed system is build using HTML, PHP, and
Javascript which is compatible to the current infrastructure and offer secure multi-user
access, role based dashboards, and report generation. The absence of automated rating
calculators, submission tracking, and document archiving are all addressed by proposed
system.

Feasibility Analysis and Managerial Impact

Technical Feasibility
         The proposed system is technically feasible given the institution's existing

infrastructure and available expertise. The development stack - HTML, PHP, JavaScript
(jQuery/Ajax), MySQL, and Bootstrap is compatible with the university's browser-based
environment and can be deployed on standard web servers. Development will be conducted
using Visual Studio Code (VS Code), which is lightweight and widely supported. The
institution's IT personnel are familiar with these technologies, ensuring adequate support
for deployment and maintenance. Faculty and administrative staff possess basic digital
literacy, and targeted training will be provided to bridge any gaps. No third-party vendors
are required, as the system will be built using open-source tools and maintained internally.
Given the alignment with current systems and the availability of technical resources, the
project is deemed technically feasible.

Economic Feasibility
         The projected cost for this project is around 15,000 which is economically

feasible. This budget includes training cost, and contingency fund. Expenses for domain
hosting will free because it will be covered by the schools current subscription. Expenses
for Licensing and Vendors fee can be avoided by using open-source technologies and
internal development. Because the system expedites the workflows, shortens performance
documentation, and lower administrative cost, the return or investment (ROI) is anticipated
during the first academic cycle. The project is economically feasible given these cost
savings and long term advantages.

Operational Feasibility
         The system is well align with the current university workflows and organizational

structure. It improve the transparency and efficiency by digitalizing the present IPCR
process without affecting essential duties. Real-time dashboards, rating calculators, and
automatic submissions will be helpful to instructors and assessors. Feedback loops,
systematic training programs, and pilot testing all help to promote user adoption. The
system embodies the institutional values of evidence-based governance, accountability,
and ongoing development. The implementation of change management tactics, such as
advocate networks, awareness campaigns, and helpdesk support, has made the system both
operationally and culturally feasible.

Legal/Ethical Feasibility
         The system adhere to the Data Privacy Act of 2012 (RA 10173), implementing

secure login protocols, encrypted data transmission and role-based access controls. It also
obtain the user consent at first login, and privacy policies are clearly explained. The rating
procedure has ethical measures to make sure that assessments are based only on authorized
IPCR indicators. Transparency and contextual reasoning are made possible via feedback
mechanisms. To guarantee continued compliance, audits and privacy impact analyses will
be carried out on a regular basis. The system is therefore morally and legally sound.

Managerial Impact
         The system will improve the managerial strategic decision making. Dean and

Program Head will be able to monitor faculty contributions and efficiently distribute
resources using automated documentation and performance analytics. With the use of
dashboard, reporting process will be more easier, and manual report consolidation will be
change to planning and data analysis. By offering organized, auditable records, the
technology facilitates institutional accreditation and CHED Institutional Sustainability
Assessment compliance. Aligned with the institution's objectives for academic excellence
and quality assurance, the system enhances managerial capability overall.

Change Management Component
         The Faculty Performance Documentation and Evaluation System successful

implementation depends on the coordinated change Management approach that takes the
organizational preparedness and individual adaptability. Key stakeholders are faculty
members, program heads and Dean. Faculty members, as primary user's may resist at first
due to possible increase of accountability and unfamiliar of the platforms. Program heads
and deans may need to adjust their monitoring and reporting procedure, even if they expect
to gain from much better workflows.
The impact of the system to the administrators or Dean will have better access to gather
data for reporting and decision-making. While the faculties will transition from manual to
automated performance tracking. Despite of the advantages, these requires a careful
adjustment.

         This approach will be guided by the ADKAR model. College briefing and
communication strategies will be used for more awareness. By showcasing the system
advantages through pilot testing and consultations, stakeholders desire to use the system
will increase. Trainings, workshops and digital modules will be utilizing for acquiring more
knowledge about the system. Lastly, Real time technical assistance, and peer coaching will
help to strengthen their ability.

         Interventions will include a centralized support center for assistance and problems,
interactive workshops, and department-level pilot programs run by faculty allies. Continual
improvements and frequent feedback gathering will guarantee that the system adapts to
user requirements. These strategies aim to reduce opposition, increase institutional trust,
and establish the system as a fundamental part of academic governance.

Requirement Specification
         The successful development and deployment of the Faculty Performance

Documentation and Evaluation System required a clear specification of both functional and
non-functional requirements. These requirements were gathered during the planning and
analysis phases through consultations with institutional stakeholders, benchmarking of
existing systems, and alignment with CHED and CSC evaluation frameworks.
Functional Requirements
    The system's functional requirements define the essential operations and services it

must provide to fulfill its intended role in documenting and evaluating faculty performance:
     Login and Registration. Enables authorized users to register and securely log into
         the system using unique credentials. Role-based access allows faculty,
         administrators, and evaluators to interact with modules relevant to their
         responsibilities.
     Faculty Dashboard and IPCR Tracking. Displays real-time evaluation status,
         visual summaries, and detailed breakdowns of submitted data. IPCR forms and
         scores are tracked across defined evaluation periods.
     Administrative Control Panel. Allows administrators to manage user accounts,
         configure evaluation periods, update criteria weights, and monitor submission
         activity. Centralized access simplifies institutional oversight.
     Evaluation Form Submission. Faculty can document achievements under
         teaching, research, service, and administrative functions. Forms support file
         attachments, draft saving, and submission confirmation.
     Performance Rating Calculator. Automatically computes evaluation scores
         based on institutional metrics and assigned weights. Supports multiple rating
         categories with aggregated totals aligned with SPMS and IPCR standards.
     Report Export and Print View. Users can generate performance summaries and
         IPCR scorecards. Reports are exportable in PDF or printer-friendly formats and
         accessible per evaluation cycle.

Non-Functional Requirements
    Non-functional requirements define the performance standards, security protocols,

and system behavior needed to ensure reliability and user satisfaction:
     System Load Speed. Web pages and dashboards must load in under two seconds
         under normal network conditions to support efficient user workflows.
     Secure Login Protocol. User authentication is protected through Secure Socket
         Layer (SSL) encryption and token-based login sessions to prevent unauthorized
         access and data breaches.
     Mobile Responsiveness. The system adapts to various screen sizes and device
         types, enabling access via desktop computers, tablets, and smartphones without
         layout distortion.
     Daily Backup and Restore Features. System data is automatically backed up each
         day to cloud storage, ensuring recovery options in case of data corruption or
         accidental deletion.
     Concurrent User Support. The platform supports up to 30 simultaneous users
         without degradation in performance, ensuring accessibility during peak submission
         periods.

         System Development Model: This project uses Iterative Waterfall Model. This

is a structured software development methodology that follows a sequential progression of
phases--requirements, design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance--
while allowing feedback and refinement between iterations. This model has been selected
because it provides a balance between structure and flexibility, which is appropriate for the
project's academic setting and the small number of the development team.

             Stakeholder Involvement: UI feedback was gathered from faculty to adjust

             Deliverables or Outputs: System Diagram, UI mockups and database design
             documentation.

             Purpose: Building the system based on the approve design.
             Activities and Procedures: Working with backend, frontend interface and
             integrated database operations.
             Tools and Techniques Used: PHP, Javascript, MySQL, HTML, VS Code and
             Bootstrap.
             Stakeholder Involvement: Giving update for milestone tracking.
             Deliverables or Outputs: Functional system modules including login, report
             submission, ratings input, and report generation.

             Purpose: To validate system functionalities, identify possible bugs, and ensure
             reliability before the deployment.
             Activities and Procedures: Conduct unit testing, integration testing, and user's
             acceptability walkthroughs.

             usability feedback.
             Stakeholder Involvement: The faculty and Dean participated on the testing and
             provide feedback for improvement.
             Deliverables or Outputs: Test case documentation, bug reports and updated
             system modules based on feedbacks.

Software Development Tools
         The Faculty Performance Documentation and Evaluation System was developed

using a suite of modern and reliable open source technologies to ensure scalability,
maintainability, and user centered design. Below are the details:

      HTML: Used for structuring web content and ensuring semantic clarity across
         pages.
      PHP: Serves as the server-side scripting language, handling backend logic,
         database interactions, and dynamic content rendering.

      JavaScript (jQuery/Ajax): Enhances client-side interactivity, supports
         asynchronous data exchange, and streamlines DOM manipulation for responsive
         user experiences.

      MySQL: A robust relational database system used to store, retrieve, and manage
         structured data related to faculty profiles, performance metrics, evaluation records,
         and system logs. It supports ACID compliance and integrates seamlessly with PHP.

      Visual Studio Code (VS Code): A lightweight yet powerful code editor offering
         syntax highlighting, version control integration (Git), and extensions for PHP,
         JavaScript, and MySQL development. It facilitated efficient coding, debugging,
         and collaboration.

      Bootstrap: A front-end framework used to design responsive and mobile-first
         interfaces. It provided pre-built UI components, grid systems, and styling utilities
         that accelerated development while ensuring visual consistency and accessibility.

Deployment Plan

         The system will be deployed using a phased rollout strategy which will begin with
a pilot implementation in selected departments before the College wide deployment. This
approach allows for controlled testing, feedback collection, and incremental adjustments,
minimizing disruption and ensuring user readiness. The pilot phase will validate core
functionalities such as report submission, rating workflows, and dashboard monitoring
before expanding access to all faculty and administrative users.

Installation Procedure: The following are the steps for system installation:
    1. Server Preparation
          Provision a secure cloud-based hosting environment
          Configure PHP, MySQL, and Apache/Nginx stack
          Enable SSL/TLS encryption for secure data transmission
    2. System Deployment
          Upload source code via FTP
          Set up database schema using MySQL scripts
          Configure environment variables and access credentials
          Test connectivity and module responsiveness
    3. User Access Setup
          Create initial admin and faculty accounts
          Assign roles and permissions based on institutional hierarchy
          Enable login portal and dashboard access
    4. Final Validation
          Conduct smoke testing to verify core functions
          Confirm report submission, rating, and documentation modules are operational
Data Migration Plan: The system migration will focus more on digitalizing the manual
and spreadsheets process of faculty records and performance evaluation.

Disaster Recovery Plan
Purpose: In the case of a significant disruption, such as a natural disaster, cyberattack, or
hardware failure, to guarantee the quick restoration of system operation and data integrity.
Both teacher performance records and institutional activities are safeguarded by this
arrangement.
Key Personnel:
Project Manager ­ Overall coordinator and decision maker.
Developer- Technical recovery and system restoration
IT Support Staff ­ Server access, backup retrieval, and network restoration
Deployment Coordinator ­ Communication with stakeholder and rollback execution
Backup Strategy:
Frequency: Daily incremental backups; weekly full backups
Location: Cloud Storage
Type: Encrypted MySQL database dumps and source code archives
Retention: 30-day rolling backup cycle with offsite replication |
Recovery Procedure:

    1. Assess the nature and scope of the disruption
    2. Inform key personnel and initiate recovery protocol
    3. Restore latest backup from cloud
    4. Reconfigure server environment and redeploy application
    5. Test system modules and verify data integrity
    6. Resume operations and notify users of restoration status
Recovery Time Objectives: Maximum acceptable downtime is 48 hours from the start of
disruption.
Recovery Point Objectives (RPO): Maximum acceptable data lose is 24 hours based on
daily backup frequency.
Testing Schedule:
     Quarterly DRP Simulation: Perform mock recovery drills quarterly
     Annual Full Recovery Test: end-to-end restoration procedures must be check.

Introduction
This document gives the users with the detailed guide to use the Faculty Performance
Documentation and Evaluation System. Faculty performance document submission,
evaluation, and reporting are made easier by the system architecture which guarantee
timeliness, transparency and adherence to institutional standards.

Getting Started
Access instruction:
 Open a web browser and type in the official system URL provided.
 Recommended browsers: Chrome, Firefox, Edge.
Login Procedure:
 Enter your assigned username and password.
 First-time users will be prompted to change their password and accept the data privacy

  agreement.
 If login fails, use the "Forgot Password" link or contact your department's system

  coordinator.

Core Functionalities
Dashboard Overview
 It displays the submission status, evaluation results, and the system notifications.
 Navigation menu includes: Home, Submit Report, View Ratings, and Export Documents.
Report Submission
Steps:

    1. Click "Submit Report" from the dashboard.
    2. Select the evaluation period and document type.
    3. Upload your completed form in PDF format.
    4. Add supporting documents if required.
    5. Click "Submit" and wait for confirmation.
Evaluation and Rating (For Program Heads/Supervisors)
Steps:
    1. Navigate to "Review Submissions."
    2. Select faculty name and evaluation period.
    3. View submitted documents.
    4. Enter ratings based on approved metrics.
    5. Save and finalize evaluation.
Report Generation
Steps:
    1. Go to "Export Documents."
    2. Choose report type (e.g., Summary, Individual Rating).
    3. Select format (PDF or Excel).
    4. Click "Generate" and download

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)
Q1: Can I edit a submitted report?
A: Yes, if the evaluation period is still open. Go to "My Submissions" and click "Edit."
Q2: Who can see my evaluation results?
A: Only authorized personnel such as your supervisor and dean.
Q3: What if I miss the submission deadline?
A: Contact your program head immediately. Late submissions may be flagged.
Q4: Is my data secure?
A: Yes. The system uses encrypted connections and role-based access controls.
